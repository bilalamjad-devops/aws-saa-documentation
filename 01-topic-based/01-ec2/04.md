4. Question

Category: CSAA – Design Resilient Architectures

A company needs to deploy at least two Amazon EC2 instances to support the normal workloads of its application and automatically scale up to six EC2 instances to handle the peak load. The architecture must be highly available and fault-tolerant as it is processing mission-critical workloads.

As a Solutions Architect, what should be done to meet this requirement?

- Create an Auto Scaling group of EC2 instances and set the minimum capacity to 2 and the maximum capacity to 6. Deploy 4 instances in Availability Zone A.
- `Create an Auto Scaling group of EC2 instances and set the minimum capacity to 4 and the maximum capacity to 6. Deploy 2 instances in Availability Zone A and another 2 instances in Availability Zone B.`
- Create an Auto Scaling group of EC2 instances and set the minimum capacity to 2 and the maximum capacity to 4. Deploy 2 instances in Availability Zone A and 2 instances in Availability Zone B.
- Create an Auto Scaling group of EC2 instances and set the minimum capacity to 2 and the maximum capacity to 6. Use 2 Availability Zones and deploy 1 instance for each AZ.


This is a **classic AWS SAA "read every word carefully" question**. Many people (including experienced AWS users) get it wrong because they focus on **Auto Scaling** and forget the requirement about **fault tolerance during an Availability Zone failure**.

Let's analyze it like an AWS Solutions Architect.

---

# Step 1: Highlight the important requirements

The question says:

> **at least two EC2 instances** to support normal workloads

Meaning:

* Under normal conditions, **minimum = 2**.

---

Then:

> automatically scale up to **six**

Meaning:

* **Maximum = 6**.

---

Then comes the most important sentence:

> **highly available and fault-tolerant**

This changes everything.

---

Then:

> **mission-critical workloads**

This tells AWS:

> We cannot afford service degradation.

---

## Notice something?

There are actually **two requirements**:

1. Minimum 2 instances during normal operation.
2. Continue operating even if an **Availability Zone fails**.

Most students only satisfy requirement #1.

AWS wants both.

---

# Step 2: What does High Availability mean?

High Availability means:

```text
AZ A        AZ B

EC2         EC2
```

If AZ A fails...

```text
AZ A ❌

AZ B
EC2
```

Application still works.

---

# Step 3: What does Fault Tolerance mean?

This is where students get confused.

Fault Tolerance is **stronger** than High Availability.

AWS definition:

The system should continue functioning **without noticeable interruption**, even if part of the infrastructure fails.

Think:

Hospital

Bank

Payment Gateway

Airline Booking

These cannot wait for new servers to boot.

---

Looks highly available.

Now imagine:

AZ A dies.

Remaining:


Question:

Answer:

Only **1**.

But the application requires:

> At least **2**

Yes.

But...

Launching EC2 takes time.

Typically:

* Boot
* Health checks
* Register with ALB

Maybe 2–5 minutes.

During those minutes...

You only have **1 instance**.

Requirement violated.

That is fault tolerance.

# SAA Cheat Sheet

| Requirement                                        | Solution                            |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| High Availability                                  | Multiple AZs                        |
| Fault Tolerance                                    | Redundant resources already running |
| Automatic recovery                                 | Auto Scaling Group                  |
| Even after AZ failure, still meet minimum capacity | Run extra instances beforehand      |

---

## One subtle point about Auto Scaling

You might wonder:

> "Can't Auto Scaling just launch new instances after an AZ fails?"

Yes, it can.

But **Auto Scaling is reactive**. It detects the failure, launches replacement instances, waits for them to boot, pass health checks, and become available. During that time, your running capacity is lower.

For **mission-critical** systems where the requirement is to **always** have at least two running instances, you don't rely on recovery—you **pre-provision redundancy**. That's the architectural principle this question is testing.

**Exam shortcut:**

* If you read **"highly available"** → think **multiple AZs**.
* If you also read **"fault tolerant"** or **"mission-critical"** → ask yourself, *"If one AZ disappears right now, do I still meet the business requirement?"*

If the answer is **no**, the design isn't fault tolerant, even if Auto Scaling can eventually recover it.
