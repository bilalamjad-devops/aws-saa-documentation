2. Question

Category: CSAA – Design Cost-Optimized Architectures

A multinational corporate and investment bank regularly processes steady workloads of accruals, loan interests, and other critical financial calculations every night from 10 PM to 3 AM on their on-premises data center for their corporate clients. Once the process is done, the results are then uploaded to the Oracle General Ledger which means that the processing should not be delayed or interrupted. The CTO has decided to move its IT infrastructure to AWS to save costs. The company needs to reserve compute capacity in a specific Availability Zone to properly run their workloads.

As the Senior Solutions Architect, how can you implement a cost-effective architecture in AWS for their financial system?

- `Use On-Demand Capacity Reservations, which provide compute capacity that is always available on the specified recurring schedule.`
- Use Regional Reserved Instances to reserve capacity on a specific Availability Zone and lower the operating cost through its billing discounts.
- Use On-Demand EC2 instances which allows you to pay for the instances that you launch and use by the second. Reserve compute capacity in a specific Availability Zone to avoid any interruption.
- Use Dedicated Hosts, which provide a physical host that is fully dedicated to running your instances, and bring your existing per-socket, per-core, or per-VM software licenses to reduce costs.

This is an **excellent SAA question** because it tests one of the most confusing AWS topics:

* On-Demand Instances
* Reserved Instances
* Capacity Reservations
* Dedicated Hosts

Many students mix these up. Let's simplify them.

---

# Step 1: Read the question carefully

The question says:

> runs every night from **10 PM to 3 AM**

That tells us:

* Not 24/7
* Predictable schedule
* Only 5 hours/day

---

Then it says:

> **processing should not be delayed or interrupted**

This is the most important requirement.

AWS keyword:

> **Guaranteed Capacity**

---

Then it says:

> reserve compute capacity in a **specific Availability Zone**

Another huge keyword.

Notice:

**specific Availability Zone**

Not Region.

Not multiple AZs.

A particular AZ.

---

# Step 2: What problem are they trying to solve?

Imagine AWS is a hotel.

You arrive every night at 10 PM.

Question:

Can AWS guarantee a room?

If you simply book when you arrive...

Maybe yes.

Maybe no.

During busy times, AWS may not have capacity.

For critical banking workloads...

That's unacceptable.

So AWS says:

> "Reserve the room in advance."

That's exactly what Capacity Reservations do.

---

# Step 3: What is Capacity Reservation?

This is the definition you should remember.

> Capacity Reservation = **Reserve hardware capacity**

NOT

> Save money

Those are two different concepts.

Many students confuse them.

---

Capacity Reservation guarantees:

"I will always have an m5.large available in us-east-1a."

Even if AWS is busy.

---

# Step 4: Why is it called "On-Demand Capacity Reservation"?

Because:

You don't commit for:

* 1 year
* 3 years

You create it.

Use it.

Delete it.

Exactly when needed.

Think:

```
10 PM
↓

Reserve Capacity

↓

Launch EC2

↓

Run Job

↓

Terminate EC2

↓

Cancel Reservation

↓

Done
```

Perfect for scheduled workloads.

---

# Step 5: Why is this the correct answer?

Question requirements:

✅ Critical workload

✅ Cannot fail

✅ Needs guaranteed capacity

✅ Runs only 5 hours/day

Capacity Reservation satisfies ALL of these.

No long-term commitment.

Guaranteed capacity.

Can automate creation/deletion.

---

# Step 6: Why Option 2 is wrong

> Regional Reserved Instance

Here's where AWS likes to trick you.

There are TWO kinds of Reserved Instances.

### Regional Reserved Instance

Benefits:

✅ Billing discount

❌ Capacity NOT guaranteed

---

### Zonal Reserved Instance

Benefits:

✅ Billing discount

✅ Capacity reservation

---

Notice the trick.

The option says:

> Regional Reserved Instance

then says

> reserve capacity in AZ

That is false.

Only **Zonal Reserved Instances** reserve capacity.

---

Easy memory trick:

Regional = Discount only

Zonal = Discount + Capacity

---

# Step 7: Why Option 3 is wrong

It says:

> Use On-Demand Instances

Many beginners think:

"I'll just start EC2 at 10 PM."

Problem:

Suppose everyone starts EC2 at 10 PM.

AWS might not have capacity.

On-Demand gives:

* flexibility

NOT

* reservation

AWS never promises:

"I'll always have hardware waiting."

---

# Step 8: Why Option 4 is wrong

Dedicated Host.

This is another service students misunderstand.

Dedicated Host means:

"You own the entire physical server."

Why?

Mostly for:

* Software licensing
* Compliance
* Oracle licensing
* Windows licensing

Example:

Your Oracle license says:

"This software must run on dedicated hardware."

Then use Dedicated Hosts.

---

Does the question mention:

* Licensing?
* Compliance?
* Bring Your Own License?

No.

Therefore:

Dedicated Host is unnecessary.

Also expensive.

---

# Step 9: Architect's View

The workload is:

```
10 PM

↓

Reserve Capacity

↓

Launch EC2

↓

Run Calculations

↓

Upload to Oracle

↓

3 AM

↓

Terminate EC2

↓

Cancel Reservation
```

No wasted compute.

Guaranteed availability.

---

# Step 10: The BIG Concept AWS Wants You to Learn

Separate these two ideas:

### Capacity

Will AWS have hardware?

Solved by:

* Capacity Reservation
* Zonal RI

---

### Discount

Can I save money?

Solved by:

* Savings Plans
* Reserved Instances

These are different problems.

AWS may ask about one.

Or both.

---

# The Four Services Compared

| Service                      | Guarantees Capacity? | Gives Discount? | Best Use Case                                                 |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| On-Demand                    | ❌                    | ❌               | Flexible, unpredictable workloads                             |
| Capacity Reservation         | ✅                    | ❌ (by itself)   | Guaranteed capacity in a specific AZ                          |
| Reserved Instance (Regional) | ❌                    | ✅               | Long-running workloads where savings matter                   |
| Reserved Instance (Zonal)    | ✅                    | ✅               | Long-running workloads that also require capacity reservation |

---

# What about Savings Plans?

Savings Plans are newer than Reserved Instances.

They:

✅ Save money

❌ Do NOT reserve capacity

Another common exam trick.

---

# Memory Trick

Think of AWS as an airline.

* **On-Demand EC2** → Buy a ticket at the airport. If seats are available, you fly.
* **Capacity Reservation** → Reserve a seat. The seat is guaranteed, but you aren't getting a discount just because you reserved it.
* **Reserved Instance / Savings Plan** → Buy a membership that gives you cheaper tickets over time.
* **Dedicated Host** → Rent the entire airplane because of a special licensing or compliance requirement.

---

# SAA Exam Cheat Sheet

Whenever you see these phrases:

* **"Guarantee capacity"**
* **"Specific Availability Zone"**
* **"Critical workload must not fail because of insufficient EC2 capacity"**

→ Think **Capacity Reservation** (or **Zonal Reserved Instance** if the scenario also emphasizes long-term cost savings).

Whenever you see:

* **"Reduce EC2 cost for workloads running continuously for 1–3 years"**

→ Think **Reserved Instances** or **Savings Plans**, depending on the flexibility described.

---

## One important clarification about this Tutorials Dojo explanation

The explanation says you can "automate" Capacity Reservations for the nightly schedule. That's true, but **On-Demand Capacity Reservations themselves are not recurring by default**. You would typically use automation (such as EventBridge and Lambda or another scheduler) to create and cancel them on a schedule. AWS also offers **Capacity Reservation Fleets** and other options for more advanced scenarios, but for the SAA exam, the key takeaway is:

> **Capacity Reservation = guaranteed EC2 capacity in a specific AZ, independent of billing discounts.**

That distinction is exactly what this question is testing.
